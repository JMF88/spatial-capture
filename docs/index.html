Two takes: one refused, one cleared

The first take — two clips, stock camera app — was refused before either clip reached the GPU. Splat training fails for reasons that are already visible in the frames — so the frames get graded first, before any GPU time is spent. The second take cleared the gate and trained.

ClipFramesExposure driftWB driftVerdict
1 241 92% 18% RESHOOT
2 182 106% 23% RESHOOT

Same two blockers each time: auto-exposure was never locked, and auto white-balance was hunting. Stock iOS AE/AF Lock does not lock white balance — which is exactly why that one is worth measuring. Drifting exposure and a wandering white point are what wreck feature matching, and neither is fixable after the fact.

The grader measures sharpness, exposure drift, white-balance drift, mains flicker, frame-to-frame overlap and clipping, then returns GO / MARGINAL / RESHOOT with the reasons named. It's advisory and says so: it can't see coverage holes or whether you closed the loop, and its overlap metric saturates below 50%. Measure first, spend later.

The second take — nine passes, shutter, ISO, white balance and focus locked, 4K/24 — cleared with zero blockers: all nine passes graded MARGINAL with advisory warnings only, none GO, none RESHOOT. Along the way the gate itself was caught wrong once — it flagged a locked-exposure take's slow brightness ramp as drift — and was fixed to measure the shape of the curve, not its size.

The grader is in the repo and under test (pipeline/rate_capture.py). The takes are not — data/ is gitignored, because they're multi-gigabyte video. Those numbers are measured results, not something you can re-run from a clone.

What the cleared take bought: COLMAP registered 354/354 frames (0.810 px mean reprojection error), Brush trained 1,342,519 Gaussians in 8.8 minutes, cleaning kept 1,019,159 (75.9%), and compression produced the 9.5 MB SOG the viewer renders. Downstream, open-vocab detection over all 354 frames (68 s on GPU, 1,564 detections) fused to 84 3D objects — typing “book” in the viewer highlights 33 of them on the actual shelf.

Two branches, two kinds of AI

One capture feeds two branches, and the interesting part is that they're two different kinds of machine learning — then they get fused into one scene.

Reconstruction — optimization

Differentiable rendering fits millions of 3D Gaussians to the photos by gradient descent. No labels, no training set — the scene is the model.

Understanding — supervision

Open-vocabulary detection, a transfer-learning classifier stage, and OCR find and read what's in the scene. Models trained on labeled examples.

The pipeline

  1. Import — the video comes off the phone over WiFi, streamed straight to disk. No cable, no cloud round-trip. It ffprobes whatever lands and warns when iOS has silently re-encoded it — a 4K60 HEVC take arrived here as 4K30 H.264: half the frames gone before reconstruction started. pipeline/00_import_server.py
  2. Frames — sharp, well-overlapped stills. pipeline/01_extract_frames.py
  3. Capture QA — grade the take. GO / MARGINAL / RESHOOT, before the GPU time. pipeline/rate_capture.py
  4. Poses — structure-from-motion: where was the camera? COLMAP by default (354/354 registered on the real capture); Postshot's free tier survives as a poses fallback, and there's an open VGGT lane. pipeline/02_poses_vggt.py
  5. Splat — fit the Gaussians. Brush (Rust/wgpu — no CUDA toolkit) by default, or gsplat (Apache-2.0 on purpose — the INRIA reference is non-commercial). Postshot was dropped from this stage: its splat export is paywalled. pipeline/03_train_gsplat.py
  6. Detect, classify, read — open-vocab detector, a transfer-learning classifier stage (validated on synthetic data so far), OCR. understanding/
  7. Fuse — camera poses lift the 2D detections into the splat's 3D frame. Pixels-with-labels become a scene graph. understanding/fusion/build_scene.py
  8. Query — type “book” and it lights up in 3D; or --json for an agent. understanding/query.py

An orchestrator runs the automatable stages from one YAML config and skips what's already built (pipeline/run.py). An eval gate blocks publishing a scene whose reconstruction, classifier or OCR metrics miss threshold (pipeline/gate.py) — the same measure-first rule, at the other end. Unit tests cover the pure logic; ruff lints; both are green locally and run on every push once the repo is published.

Public code, private room

GitHub Pages has no access control — a public repo serves public files, full stop. But the thing worth showing is a scan of a real, personal space. So the boundary goes in the file rather than the host: the asset ships as AES-256-GCM ciphertext, and the viewer decrypts it in the browser with a passphrase via WebCrypto. No server, no auth provider, no third party. Whoever has the phrase sees the room; everyone else reads the code, which stays entirely public.

The limits are the honest part. It isn't revocable — the ciphertext is public and permanent, and forks outlive any later deletion. It's no stronger than the passphrase, since an attacker has the blob and unlimited offline guesses; PBKDF2 makes each guess expensive, not impossible. It keeps a private room out of search engines and gates it behind something handed to one person. It is not a secrets manager.

Trove

Trove is the capture app — an installable PWA: a guided capture coach (object vs. enclosure), one-tap WiFi import to the workstation, a collection of scenes, and a catalog of every object found across them.

It is deliberately not a camera. On iOS a web page can't open the Camera app or lock a look for it: WebKit exposes only white balance, zoom and torch to web code, while exposure, focus and ISO are unimplemented. Recording in-page runs all three in continuous auto — which is precisely the drift the QA gate above caught. So Trove coaches the shot, you take it in an app with real manual control, and Trove carries the file to the GPU.

Why enclosures

Most real reality-capture jobs — training sims, workspace walkthroughs, digital twins — are enclosures: you stand inside and the camera looks out. Divergent views give the solver less overlap to lock onto, so inside-out is the harder case than orbiting an object — which is exactly why it's the valuable one. That's the case this pipeline is built for, and the one it hasn't proven yet: the reconstructed shelf was the de-risking subject; the enclosure is next.
